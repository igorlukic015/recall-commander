:::rc-prompt

What is the primary function of the mitochondria in a cell?

:::

:::rc-answer

The mitochondria produce most of the cell's ATP through aerobic respiration.

They are often described as the powerhouse of the cell.

Some notes about the difference between the two main cell types.

---

:::rc-question

type: Recall

concepts:
- Cell Biology
- Prokaryotes
- Eukaryotes

:::rc-prompt

What is the key structural difference between a prokaryotic and a eukaryotic cell?

:::

:::rc-answer

Eukaryotic cells have a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

Prokaryotic cells have neither; their genetic material floats freely in the cytoplasm.

:::

What are the four nucleotide bases found in DNA?

:::

:::rc-answer

Adenine pairs with thymine, and guanine pairs with cytosine.

More notes about cellular processes and energy.

---

:::rc-question

type: Explanation

concepts:
- Cellular Respiration
- ATP
- Metabolism

:::rc-prompt

Explain how cellular respiration converts glucose into usable energy.

Your answer should mention the main stages and the role of oxygen.

:::

:::rc-answer

Cellular respiration breaks down glucose in three main stages: glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and the electron transport chain.

Glycolysis splits glucose into pyruvate in the cytoplasm, producing a small amount of ATP. The citric acid cycle further oxidizes the products in the mitochondrial matrix, releasing electron carriers. The electron transport chain uses those carriers and oxygen as the final electron acceptor to generate the bulk of the cell's ATP.

Oxygen is essential because without it the electron transport chain halts and the cell must fall back on far less efficient anaerobic pathways.

:::

:::

concepts:
- Genetics
- Meiosis
- Genetic Variation

:::rc-prompt

Explain how meiosis produces genetic variation.

:::

:::rc-answer

Meiosis introduces variation through two main mechanisms: independent assortment and crossing over.

During metaphase I, homologous chromosome pairs line up and are distributed to daughter cells independently of one another, producing many possible combinations. During prophase I, homologous chromosomes exchange segments through crossing over, creating new combinations of alleles on a single chromosome.

Together with the random fusion of gametes at fertilization, these processes generate enormous genetic diversity.

:::
